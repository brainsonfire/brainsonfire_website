The Fitness Rebellion

next story

Backdrop

When the owners of a fitness empire write a book called “Working Out Sucks,” you know they’re not your average fitness gurus. Meet Dave Mortensen and Chuck Runyon, owners and founders of Anytime Fitness. Driven by a vision to “improve the self-esteem of the world,” Chuck and Dave recognized that the traditional fitness conversation had hit a wall. Sure, the “buns and guns” conversation was appealing to traditional gym rats, but what about the overwhelming majority of Americans? In a country where only 15% of people belong to a gym (and only 60% of the remaining 85% are even willing to consider going to one), it was painfully obvious that it was time for someone to stand up and start a new kind of fitness conversation.

This new conversation would speak to the 85% by shining a spotlight on the accomplishments of people just like them. Instead of glorifying hundred-pound weight loss stories, marathons and Iron Mans, Anytime Fitness would celebrate little wins like clocking an extra five minutes on the treadmill, resisting the urge to hit the snooze button and the first time a new member mustered up the courage to walk through the front doors of the gym.

Purpose

Reframe the fitness conversation by talking about Little Wins and celebrating everyday accomplishments. Foster a stronger internal relationship between Anytime Fitness corporate, their owners and trainers. Empower Anytime Fitness O2is (owners) and H2is (trainers) with ways to deepen their relationships with members and their local communities by recognizing accomplishments, however small. Lower the barrier of entry by establishing Anytime Fitness as the voice behind a new kind of fitness conversation

Success

Hundreds of Success Stories and Little Wins have been submitted by Anytime Fitness members and non-members and featured on FitnessRebellion.com

Thousands of people have joined the new fitness conversation on Facebook and Twitter

The Fitness Rebellion has received national media attention including mentions on WebMD, The Huffington Post and The Doctors.

COMMUNITY LEADERSHIP

TRAINING HANDBOOK

Changing the traditional fitness conversation wasn’t going to be easy. In order to make it work, we knew we’d need leaders who really got it. They’d have to be willing to take a big step away from the fitness world they’d always known, and courageous enough to start an entirely new and meaningful conversation nobody else was having. We put out a call for internal advocates. After reviewing 100 applications and clocking some serious interview time on Skype, 25 Anytime Fitness owners and trainers rose to the top of our list. We had found our Fitness Rebellion Leaders. Dotting the map from Gig Harbor, Washington to Clearwater, Florida, each Fitness Rebellion Leader brought their own unique story and perspective to the Fitness Rebellion.

WELCOME TO THE FITNESS REBELLION

RECRUITMENT KIT
At the onset of the recruitment process, Anytime Fitness identified several owners and trainers they felt would make outstanding Fitness Rebellion Leaders. These handpicked recruits received a surprise Fitness Rebellion kit in the mail encouraging them to check out The Fitness Rebellion mission and submit an application if they felt inspired to help bring the movement to life.

WELCOME CARD + BUTTON
After reading The Fitness Rebellion Manifesto and officially joining the community, new members receive a Fitness Rebellion button attached to a handwritten welcome note. This small token has become a symbol of belonging that Fitness Rebels around the country proudly display everywhere from gym bags to t-shirts, dog leashes to rearview mirrors as a reminder of their commitment to self.

YEAH, I’D WORK OUT...BUT.

We all know we should work out, BUT are a million reasons and excuses to skip it. I should work out, BUT my iPod batteries are almost dead. I should work out, BUT the game is on this afternoon. I should work out, BUT I left my ponytail holder at home. The Fitness Rebellion confronted the BUT head on. Fitness Rebellion Leaders encouraged their staff to recognize little wins in the club by handing out “Kicking BUT” cards. These moments became a celebration of personal victory when members could have listened to their “BUT,” but refused to give in.
